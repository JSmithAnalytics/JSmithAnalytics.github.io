I found datasets on Kaggle for solar and wind power generation by country that I thought would make an interesting comparison. Both were attributed to Wikipedia. There was some data missing from the solar dataset on Kaggle, so for that dataset I copy/pasted off Wikipedia into a .csv file rather than using the one on Kaggle. I thought it would be interesting to then also consider CO2 emissions to compare the top CO2 emitting countries and the top solar/wind producing countries. I found a dataset on Kaggle, but after inspecting it I questioned the accuracy, so I found a table on worldometers.info that I used instead. Last, I used a table containing the populations by country that was from the same Wikipedia page as the wind dataset.

 

I liked how the solar data contained both new and total capacity for each year, so before loading into MySQL, I added the new installed columns for the wind data and computed those numbers as well. Then I loaded all my datasets, found that the spaces in the column names were problematic, edited the column names to eliminate the spaces, and loaded them again. I ran quite several queries, iterating on them as I thought of questions to ask about the data. Once I thought I had all the data that would answer my questions, I exported 6 result tables as .csv files and then uploaded them into Power BI.

 

I played with many visualizations in Power BI, most of which didnt reveal anything particularly interesting about the data. I tried bars, lines, maps, even a pie. I wanted to try some types of visualizations that werent included in the stock set, so I downloaded several 3rd party visuals, including a slope graph, a tornado, a dumbbell, a bullet, and a side-by-side bar. The slope graph didnt seem to work, the tornado showed my data nicely, but the options to format were limited and I couldnt figure out how to add a legend. The bullet was interesting but not great for my data. The dumbbell and side-by-side were very successful but, in the end, I didnt include the dumbbell in my final selections because I felt others told my story the more clearly. I ended up with three different types of bar charts that are shown below.

The first visualization (Figure 1) shows the total wind and solar energy production capacity by country as of 2020. I chose a stacked bar for this one because I wanted to show the total capacity but also the mix of solar and wind within the total. I had to Google how to make a stacked bar sort by total instead of one of the categories (add a total column to the tooltips field), and once I figured that out, I was pleased with how it came out.

I like that it shows the massive difference in the production of China next to even the US, and I also like how you can see which countries are far more invested in one technology than the other (solar dominates in Japan and Vietnam, wind in Canada and Sweden).

 

I chose the colors because they are soft and muted, and clearly different but harmonious. I also thought the violet felt airy to evoke wind and the yellow is an obvious nod to solar. The SQL query this visualization depicts is Figure 4.

The second visualization (Figure 2) shows the combined growth of solar/wind power by country from 2016 to 2020 and displays the changes both in absolute terms (as Megawatts added) and in percentage growth. When I was exploring the dataset, I noticed an enormous difference between when I displayed the total MW added or the percentage added. I thought this was an interesting story, but it was challenging to depict in one visual because of the drastically different scales. I started with a bar and line graph but felt the jagged lines might be misleading because lines usually imply passage through time, but this data is categorical. I finally found that I could keep the bar/line graph but could format the lines to make them appear as bars. The effect is an overlapping bar chart that shows both the absolute and percentage changes.

 

I wanted to keep the colors in the same family because theyre simply different measures of the same data. I landed on an opaque yellow-leaning blue for the absolute Megawatts because I felt an opaque color was appropriate for the more solid absolute measure, and a transparent, red-leaning blue for the percentage because I thought the transparency reflects the more abstract nature of percentages. I included a line marker on top of the percentage bars to make the tops easier to see and compare, especially with those that dont rise above the opaque bars.

 

I think this chart is successful because it shows not only the enormous addition of Megawatts made by China, but also how small that growth is relative to their existing capacity, and it highlights the investments made by smaller countries like Vietnam, whose addition looks tiny compared to Chinas, but relative to their size is astronomical. The one thing that I dont like is the country labels being rotated, but I couldnt find a way to avoid that. The SQL query this visualization depicts is Figure 5.

The last chart (Figure 3) was one of the last ones I created. I was still exploring the data and trying different visualizations when I discovered the Share_of_World column in the CO2 dataset, containing each countrys percentage share of the worlds total CO2 emissions. I wasnt originally planning to use this column and had forgotten about it, but then decided I wanted to compare the share of emissions against the share of solar/wind power produced by each country. Unfortunately, neither the solar nor wind datasets contained that information, and the CO2 data was in a separate table. Rather than going back to MySQL and running a new query, however, I decided to see if Power BI could take the final steps for me and was successful. As such, this is my only visualization that pulls data from two separate tables (Figures 6-7). Finally, a simple setting to display the solar/wind data as a % of the total gave me what I wanted. I tried the data in both a dumbbell chart and the side-by-side bar chart, and while both told the story, it was easier to see without explanation in the side-by-side bar.

 

I think this visualization is successful because it highlights several interesting insights. One is that the top two countries in share of CO2 emissions are also the top two in solar/wind capacity, almost to the exact percentage share. It also highlights some countries that emit a much higher share of CO2 than their share of solar/wind production (India, Russia, Iran), and more countries that produce a far greater share of solar/wind power than their share of CO2 emissions (Germany, Japan, UK, Italy, Spain).

 

For this visualization, I kept the same blue for the solar/wind capacity that I had on the previous visual for continuity, then added a grayish brown for the CO2 emissions. I think it works nicely because both colors are still muted and harmonious, but the brown evokes the pollution of CO2, and the blue implies the virtue of clean energy.

Assignment Questions:

a. How is visualizing data and querying a database similar?

 

Visualizing data and querying a database are similar in that they both use analytical techniques to make sense of a dataset. In both cases we begin with a pile of data that is essentially meaningless, and we use various techniques to find answers or information that contribute to a story or stories about and within the data.

 

b. How are they distinct?

 

They are distinct in that when querying a database, we must know exactly what we want the query to deliver because the very nature of a query is that it asks a specific question. Visualization, on the other hand, can sometimes be used to answer a specific question, but it can also be used in a more exploratory capacity. Often trends or relationships in data are difficult if not impossible to spot in the raw data, but when we use visualization to display the shapes of distribution or the slopes of change over time, patterns can become instantly clear.

 

c. Under what circumstances should you do each?

 

Querying a database can be used in a couple circumstances. First, it should be used to answer a question that is very narrow and specific, such as Which customer placed the largest order last month?. Alternatively, it can also be used to gather, organize, and clean a subset of data for further analysis or visualization. For example, if we want to look for trends within the lighting category over the last 18 months, we could query the broader sales database to return all transactions that fit those parameters. The results could then be exported into Excel, Power BI, or another analysis tool.

 

Visualization has two parts. First, it can be used to explore a large dataset, looking for meaningful patterns or relationships by looking at the distribution, rank/proportion, change over time, relationships between variables, and geographical makeup. Then, once a story about the data is clearly established, visualization can be used to communicate that story. This is when care must be taken to ensure that the visualization is designed to tell the story as clearly as possible.
